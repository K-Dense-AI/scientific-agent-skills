# Cognitive Science Experimental Design

## Purpose

This skill provides expert guidance for designing experimental paradigms across cognitive science and neuroscience research domains. It consolidates methodological knowledge for cognitive tasks, developmental methods, psycholinguistics, visual attention, creativity assessment, and social cognition. A general-purpose programmer could implement basic experiments, but would not know domain-specific timing parameters, age-appropriate methods, validated scoring systems, or critical design constraints that determine whether an experiment yields interpretable data.

This is a **router skill** that directs you to specialized sub-skills based on your research question, population, and domain.

## When to Use

Use this skill when:

- Designing a new cognitive or neuroscience experiment
- Selecting an appropriate experimental paradigm for a research question
- Configuring timing parameters, trial structures, and stimulus properties
- Adapting paradigms for specific populations (infants, children, adults, clinical groups)
- Planning stimulus norming, scoring procedures, or quality control measures
- Ensuring methodological rigor in experimental design

Do not use this skill when:

- Analyzing existing data (use analysis-specific skills)
- Designing non-experimental studies (surveys, correlational, neuroimaging analysis)
- Working with paradigms outside cognitive/neuroscience domains

## Research Planning Protocol

Before executing the domain-specific steps below, you MUST:

1. **State the research question** — What specific question is this experimental paradigm addressing?
2. **Justify the method choice** — Why this paradigm? What alternatives were considered?
3. **Declare expected outcomes** — What results would support vs. refute the hypothesis?
4. **Note assumptions and limitations** — What does this method assume? Where could it mislead?
5. **Present the plan to the user and WAIT for confirmation** before proceeding.

For detailed methodology guidance, see the `research-literacy` skill.

## Domain-Specific Guidance

### Infant Looking Time Methods

**Population**: Infants 0-24 months

**Paradigms**:
- Habituation (criterion-based or fixed-trial)
- Preferential looking (side-by-side comparison)
- Violation-of-expectation (VoE)

**Key Parameters by Age**:

| Age | Max Trial Duration | Look-Away Criterion | Habituation Trials |
|-----|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 0-1 mo | 60 s | 2-3 s | 8-12 |
| 3-6 mo | 20-30 s | 2 s | 6-10 |
| 6-12 mo | 15-20 s | 1-2 s | 6-8 |
| 12-24 mo | 10-20 s | 1-2 s | 4-8 |

**Critical Design Decisions**:
- Habituation criterion: 50% decline from baseline (first 3 trials) over 3 consecutive trials
- Novelty vs. familiarity preference depends on age, stimulus complexity, and encoding time (Hunter & Ames, 1988)
- Expected exclusion rate: 20-40% in-lab, 30-50% online
- Inter-coder reliability: Cohen's kappa > 0.85, Pearson r > 0.90

**When to Use**:
- Testing infant knowledge representations
- Measuring discrimination abilities
- Assessing spontaneous preferences

**Full details**: `references/infant-looking-time-designer.md`

### Theory of Mind Assessment

**Population**: Children (3+ years) and adults; clinical populations (ASD, brain injury, dementia)

**Task Selection by Age**:

| Age Range | Recommended Tasks | Construct |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| 3-5 years | Wellman & Liu Scale, Sally-Anne, Unexpected Contents | First-order false belief |
| 6-9 years | Second-order false belief, Faux pas stories | Recursive mental states |
| 9+ years / Adults | Strange Stories, RMET, Director task, MASC | Advanced ToM, pragmatics |

**Task Selection by Population**:
- **Autism (children)**: Sally-Anne, Unexpected Contents, Strange Stories (avoid ceiling effects)
- **Autism (adults)**: RMET, Faux pas, MASC, Director task
- **Neurological patients**: Faux pas, Strange Stories, RMET (sensitive to frontal/right hemisphere lesions)
- **Aging research**: Faux pas, RMET, Strange Stories (control for processing speed)

**Critical Considerations**:
- Control for language ability (PPVT or verbal IQ)
- Control for executive function (inhibition, working memory)
- Include physical causality control stories
- Report ceiling/floor effects and score distributions
- Minimum N: 20 for simple contrasts, 30-40 for factorial designs

**When to Use**:
- Assessing belief attribution, emotion recognition, or social reasoning
- Comparing clinical and typical populations
- Studying developmental trajectories of social cognition

**Full details**: `references/tom-task-selector.md`

### Psycholinguistic Methods

**Domains**: Sentence processing, lexical access, semantic integration

**Primary Methods**:

1. **Self-Paced Reading (SPR)**
   - Standard for behavioral sentence processing
   - Non-cumulative moving window (most common)
   - Maze task (reduced spillover, better for web-based studies)
   - Time limit: Self-paced (in-lab) or 3000-5000 ms timeout (online)

2. **Sentence Stimulus Norming**
   - Cloze probability: Minimum 30 raters per item (preferred 40-50)
   - Plausibility ratings: 7-point Likert, minimum 20 raters
   - Lexical controls: SUBTLEX-US frequency, word length, orthographic neighborhood
   - Latin square counterbalancing for within-item designs

**Critical SPR Design Requirements**:
- Match critical regions for word length (±1 character) and log frequency
- Include 2-3 spillover words after critical region (spillover is the primary data pattern)
- Avoid critical regions at clause/sentence boundaries (wrap-up effects)
- Minimum 24 items per condition (32-40 for subtle effects or L2 speakers)
- Filler-to-experimental ratio: 2:1 or 3:1
- Comprehension questions on 1/3 to 1/2 of trials, not targeting the manipulation

**When to Use SPR**:
- Studying incremental sentence comprehension
- Testing syntactic or semantic processing effects
- Budget-friendly alternative to eye-tracking
- Web-based data collection (especially Maze task)

**Full details**: `references/self-paced-reading-designer.md`, `references/sentence-stimulus-norming.md`

### Visual Search Design

**Domain**: Visual attention, feature binding, search efficiency

**Search Types**:

| Type | Search Slope | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Feature search | < 10 ms/item | Red among green |
| Conjunction search | 20-30 ms/item | Red vertical among red horizontal + green vertical |
| Spatial configuration | 30-50+ ms/item | T among Ls |

**Key Parameters**:

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Maximum eccentricity | 15 degrees | Beyond this, acuity drops substantially |
| Minimum inter-item spacing | > 1 degree | Prevents crowding (Bouma's law: 0.5 × eccentricity) |
| Item size | 0.5-2 degrees | Standard range |
| Set sizes | 4, 8, 12, 16 (minimum 3) | Need multiple points for slope estimation |
| Target-present : absent ratio | 1:1 (50%) | Standard; low prevalence (<25%) increases miss rate |
| Trials per cell | 20-30 minimum | For stable RT distributions |

**Critical Design Decisions**:
- Control eccentricity confounds: Use fixed display area, not expanding area
- Equate luminance across colors (use photometer or isoluminant settings)
- Counterbalance target position across quadrants and eccentricity bins
- For conjunction search: Ensure equal numbers of each distractor type

**When to Use**:
- Testing feature integration theory predictions
- Measuring attentional guidance
- Studying target-distractor similarity effects
- Investigating prevalence effects in applied contexts

**Full details**: `references/visual-search-array-generator.md`

### Creativity Assessment

**Domains**: Divergent thinking, creative ideation, AI-augmented creativity

**Primary Methods**:

1. **Alternative Uses Task (AUT)**
   - Time limit: 5 minutes per object
   - Standard objects: Brick, paperclip, newspaper, cardboard box
   - Response format: Open-ended text, one use per line
   - Instructions: "Generate unusual, creative, uncommon uses"

2. **Divergent Thinking Scoring**
   - **Fluency**: Count of valid, non-redundant responses
   - **Flexibility**: Number of distinct conceptual categories (COWA system)
   - **Originality**: Statistical rarity (<5% threshold) or subjective rating (1-5 scale)
   - **Semantic distance**: GloVe cosine distance (automated via SemDis platform)

3. **Mediation Analysis** (for AI-augmented creativity studies)
   - Mediator: Creative self-efficacy (3-item scale, α = 0.83-0.89)
   - Moderator: Baseline creativity (RAT, 15 items)
   - Analysis: SEM with bootstrap CIs (5000 samples)

**AI-Augmented Design** (Lee & Chung, 2024):
- Conditions: ChatGPT, Web Search, No Assistance (between-subjects)
- Sample size: 100-200 per condition
- Platform: Qualtrics + Prolific/MTurk
- Attention checks: 1-2 instructed-response items
- Exclusion: Failed attention check, <2 responses, <3 min completion time

**Critical Considerations**:
- Handle fluency-originality confound: Use top-2 scoring or ratio-based originality
- Inter-rater reliability for subjective scoring: ICC ≥ 0.70 (acceptable), ≥ 0.80 (good)
- Normalize text before computing statistical rarity (spelling, phrasing)
- For semantic distance: Clean data before averaging (remove gibberish)

**When to Use**:
- Measuring divergent thinking ability
- Testing interventions or manipulations on creativity
- Studying AI's impact on human creative output
- Investigating psychological mechanisms (self-efficacy, baseline ability)

**Full details**: `references/alternative-uses-task-designer.md`, `references/divergent-thinking-scoring.md`, `references/creativity-self-efficacy-mediation.md`

### Cognitive Task Design

**Domains**: Attention, memory, executive function, decision making, perception

**Paradigm Selection by Construct**:

| Construct | Paradigm | Key Measure |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Selective attention | Stroop, Flanker | RT interference effect |
| Spatial orienting | Posner cueing | Validity effect (invalid - valid RT) |
| Response inhibition | Stop-signal task | SSRT (stop-signal reaction time) |
| Cognitive flexibility | Task switching | Switch cost (switch - repeat RT) |
| Working memory capacity | Change detection | Cowan's K |
| False memory | DRM paradigm | False recall/recognition rate |
| Perceptual threshold | QUEST, Staircase | Threshold estimate (75% correct) |
| Temporal discounting | Delay discounting | Indifference points, k parameter |

**General Timing Guidelines**:

| Parameter | Default | Adjustment |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| Stimulus duration | Until response or 100-500 ms | Shorten for masking; lengthen for clinical populations |
| ISI/ITI | 1000-2000 ms | Increase to 2000-3000 ms for EEG; jitter 2-8 s for fMRI |
| Response deadline | 1500-2000 ms | Tighten for speed emphasis; loosen for accuracy emphasis |

**Trial Count Guidelines**:

| Effect Size | Minimum Trials per Condition |
|-------------|----------------------------|
| Large (d > 0.8) | 40-60 |
| Medium (d ~ 0.5) | 60-100 |
| Small (d ~ 0.3) | 100-200 |
| SDT measures | 100+ total (50+ signal, 50+ noise) |

**Critical Design Principles**:
- Within-subjects preferred for most cognitive paradigms (maximizes power)
- Counterbalance stimulus-response mappings and response hand
- Practice trials: 10-20 for simple tasks, 20-40 for complex tasks, require >80% accuracy
- RT trimming: Remove <200 ms (anticipatory) and >2.5 SD or >2000 ms (slow)
- Always report both RT and accuracy

**When to Use**:
- Testing core cognitive processes
- Comparing clinical and control groups
- Measuring individual differences in cognitive abilities
- Validating computational models (e.g., drift-diffusion)

## Cross-Domain Considerations

### Online vs. In-Lab Testing

| Factor | In-Lab | Online |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Environmental control | High | Low (home distractions) |
| Timing precision | High (ms-level) | Moderate (browser variability) |
| Sample diversity | Limited (local) | Broad (geographic, demographic) |
| Exclusion rate | 10-30% | 30-50% |
| Cost per participant | High (space, equipment) | Low (participant payment only) |

**Online-Appropriate Paradigms**:
- Self-paced reading (especially Maze task)
- Alternative Uses Task
- Visual search (with attention checks)
- Theory of Mind questionnaires (RMET, Strange Stories)
- Infant looking time (webcam-based, higher exclusion)

**Online Quality Control**:
- Attention checks: 10-15% catch trials
- Completion time: Exclude <50% of median time
- Straight-lining: Exclude zero variance in ratings
- Bot detection: reCAPTCHA
- Native speaker verification: Self-report + language background

### Sample Size Planning

**General Guidelines**:
- Infant studies: N = 16-24 per condition (high exclusion rate)
- Developmental comparisons: N = 20-30 per age group
- Adult cognitive tasks: N = 24-40 per condition (within-subjects)
- Individual differences: N = 100+ for correlational analyses
- Clinical vs. control: N = 30-50 per group (between-subjects)

**Power Considerations**:
- Small effects (d ~ 0.3): Require N = 100+ per condition
- Reliability-critical measures (SSRT, K): Require 160-200 trials
- Mediation analysis: N = 100-200 per condition for adequate power

### Reporting Standards

**Minimum Checklist** (applicable across paradigms):
- [ ] Paradigm type and theoretical rationale
- [ ] Population: age (mean, range), inclusion/exclusion criteria
- [ ] Sample size with power justification or sensitivity analysis
- [ ] Stimulus parameters: timing, size (in degrees of visual angle), presentation method
- [ ] Trial structure: number per condition, counterbalancing scheme
- [ ] Exclusion criteria and number excluded (with reasons)
- [ ] Dependent variables and analysis plan (pre-registered if applicable)
- [ ] Effect sizes with confidence intervals
- [ ] For norming: N raters, inter-rater reliability (ICC, kappa)
- [ ] For online studies: platform, pay rate, attention checks, exclusion rates
- [ ] Full materials in supplementary materials or online repository

## Common Pitfalls Across Domains

1. **Insufficient stimulus control**: Failing to match lexical properties, luminance, or low-level perceptual features across conditions
2. **Inadequate sample size**: Underpowered studies cannot detect medium or small effects reliably
3. **Missing practice trials**: Participants unfamiliar with task mechanics introduce noise
4. **Poor counterbalancing**: Systematic confounds from stimulus order, response mappings, or condition order
5. **Ignoring exclusion rates**: High exclusion (>40%) may bias the sample toward high-performing participants
6. **Not reporting reliability**: Inter-rater reliability (for coding) or internal consistency (for scales) must be reported
7. **Ceiling/floor effects**: Age-inappropriate tasks or poorly calibrated difficulty levels
8. **Confounding population differences**: Not controlling for language, IQ, or executive function in developmental/clinical studies
9. **Inadequate norming**: Using experimenter intuition instead of empirical cloze, plausibility, or frequency data
10. **Missing control conditions**: No baseline or active control to rule out alternative explanations
